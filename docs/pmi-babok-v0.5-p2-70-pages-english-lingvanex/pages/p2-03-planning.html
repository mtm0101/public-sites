P2.4.1 Conduct Stakeholder Analysis (Tiến hành phân tích các bên liên quan)

What it is

Stakeholder analysis interprets stakeholder influence, interest, expectations, information needs, decision authority, and potential resistance. It gives the BA a structured way to reduce ambiguity and make product information usable by the wider team.

Why it is used

For P2-4-1, the value is to keep business goals, stakeholder intent, product scope, and delivery decisions aligned. Without it, teams may deliver quickly while still solving the wrong problem.

When to use it

Use it after initial identification and before choosing engagement or communication strategies. Use P2-4-1 with extra care when the next decision may affect investment, scope, priority, design, testing, transition, or release confidence.

How to apply it

For P2-4-1, begin with the decision to support, inspect the available inputs, choose a technique that fits the uncertainty, create a reviewable output, and connect the result to the next BA or delivery activity.

Use the output in backlog decisions, models, requirements, traceability, acceptance, release, or value measurement.

Step-by-step application guide

  1. Frame the decision. State what the team needs to decide and what risk will remain if the decision is made with weak information.
  2. Inspect the inputs. Separate confirmed facts from assumptions, old documentation, stakeholder preferences, and missing evidence.
  3. Select a technique. Match the P2-4-1 method to the information need: discovery, comparison, modeling, prioritization, validation, or approval.
  4. Create a usable artifact. Keep the artifact clear enough for review and traceable enough to survive future changes.
  5. Close the loop. Confirm the result, record open questions, and update the appropriate product-information repository.

Practical example

A sponsor needs benefit and investment information, QA needs acceptance clarity, operations needs support impact, and users need workflow scenarios.

Common anti-patterns

  • Treating conduct stakeholder analysis as a form-filling exercise instead of a decision-support activity.
  • Failing to name assumptions, owners, and unresolved questions before the next handoff.
  • Creating a P2-4-1 artifact that is not reviewed, traced, or reused by product, delivery, testing, or release stakeholders.

  • Which technique best matches the type of uncertainty?
  • Who must review or approve the output?
  • Where will the result be traced, updated, or used next?

Agile / hybrid BA lens

In an Agile environment, P2-4-1 may appear as refinement notes, story-map updates, a decision record, a prototype annotation, or acceptance criteria. In a predictive or compliance-heavy environment, P2-4-1 may require a more formal document and approval path. The form should change with context; the reasoning should remain explicit.

Key takeaway: Conduct Stakeholder Analysis is valuable when it produces evidence that stakeholders actually use to decide, prioritize, design, test, transition, or release.
