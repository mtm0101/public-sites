Chapter 1: Introduction (Giới thiệu)

Chapter 1 positions business analysis as a discipline that helps an organization understand needs, connect those needs to product information, and evaluate whether a delivered solution creates business value.

Body content is English-only. Titles and terminology remain bilingual.

1.1 Overview and Purpose of This Guide (Tổng quan và mục đích của hướng dẫn này)

What it is

Establishes a shared frame for understanding and applying business analysis across portfolios, programs, projects, and product work. In practical BA work, this section turns vague observations into structured information that another person can review, test, or use in a product decision.

Why it matters

It reduces the risk that different teams use different meanings for needs, requirements, solutions, and business value. When it is performed well, the team reduces misunderstanding, avoids late rework, and increases the chance that the solution is connected to business value rather than only backlog completion.

When to use it

When an organization is starting to standardize ba capability, launching a new initiative, or aligning several functions around a common way of working. It becomes especially important when assumptions are unvalidated, when multiple stakeholder groups see the situation differently, or when the next decision will consume meaningful resources.

How to apply it

Start with the decision question: what does the team need to know in order to move forward safely? Then identify sources, choose an appropriate technique, document the result at the right level of detail, and confirm the result with people who have authority or direct experience.

Step-by-step application guide

  1. Frame the question. Write one sentence describing the decision to be supported; do not begin with the name of a preferred solution.
  2. Gather evidence. Combine documents, data, interviews, observations, or workshops to avoid relying on a single opinion.
  3. Structure the information. Use a table, model, story map, RACI, capability view, or decision log to expose relationships and dependencies.
  4. Confirm and adjust. Send the result back to the right stakeholders; separate confirmed facts, assumptions, and unresolved decisions.
  5. Connect to delivery. Convert the result into a backlog item, acceptance criteria, roadmap item, risk, action, or metric so it is not lost.

Practical example

In a healthcare software project, a sponsor says that provider search must improve. Product management may think about ranking, engineering may think about Azure Search, and QA may think about test data. A BA uses the purpose of the guide to separate three layers: the business need is helping users find the right provider; product information includes search criteria, data, and constraints; the solution is the set of product changes the team eventually chooses.

Common mistakes

  • reading the guide as a checklist of documents instead of a decision-support framework
  • treating the BA as only a note taker or requirement writer
  • failing to connect BA work to measurable business value

Application checklist

  • Is the problem or opportunity clearly described?
  • Do stakeholders use the same meaning for key terms?
  • Does the BA output support an investment, prioritization, or design decision?
  • Is there a way to evaluate value after release?

BA lens in Agile or hybrid work

In Agile delivery, this content does not have to become a long document. It can appear in a story map, refinement note, prototype annotation, decision record, or acceptance criteria. In hybrid or predictive delivery, the same information may need a more formal form for approval, audit, or handoff. The quality criterion is not document size; it is whether the information is reliable, current, and useful for the next decision.

Key takeaway: Overview and Purpose of This Guide should produce evidence that helps the team decide, prioritize, design, test, or transition. If the artifact is not used, reduce its weight or change how it is presented.

1.2 Foundational Elements (Yếu tố nền tảng)

What it is

Clarifies the relationship among products, projects, life cycles, business value, and measures of success. In practical BA work, this section turns vague observations into structured information that another person can review, test, or use in a product decision.

Why it matters

It prevents the backlog from becoming a disconnected wish list and keeps each item connected to a larger business outcome. When it is performed well, the team reduces misunderstanding, avoids late rework, and increases the chance that the solution is connected to business value rather than only backlog completion.

When to use it

When multiple initiatives compete for funding, when one product is delivered through several projects, or when the team must explain why a feature is worth the investment. It becomes especially important when assumptions are unvalidated, when multiple stakeholder groups see the situation differently, or when the next decision will consume meaningful resources.

Practical example

A team wants to add in-app messaging. The BA does not stop at the feature name. The BA asks whether the business wants to reduce coordination time, increase response rates, or improve user experience. Once the value is explicit, the team can decide whether chat, notifications, workflow changes, or better guidance is the most appropriate solution.

Common mistakes

  • equating project success with delivering all scope
  • confusing a product with the project that delivers part of it
  • choosing metrics that are easy to collect but weakly related to value

Application checklist

  • Have output, outcome, and benefit been separated?
  • Who receives value and who experiences the change?
  • Does the delivery life cycle fit the uncertainty level of the product?
  • Is there a plan to measure value after implementation?

End-of-chapter quiz

Question 1 - Hard

A Chapter 1 initiative has stakeholders using different meanings for the same product goal. What should the BA do first to reduce rework risk?

  1. A. Ask only the sponsor because the sponsor has the highest authority
  2. B. Write all requirements immediately so engineering can begin
  3. C. Wait for QA to detect gaps after development is complete
  4. D. Create a shared view, confirm assumptions, and connect the output to the next decision
Show answer

Correct answer: D

The strongest action creates clarity and validates assumptions before they become scope.

Question 2 - Hard

In the context of Introduction, which behavior shows that the guide is being applied too mechanically?

  1. A. The technique changes when new evidence appears
  2. B. Stakeholders are invited based on role and influence
  3. C. Artifacts are selected according to risk and life cycle
  4. D. Every project must use the same documentation set regardless of risk
Show answer

Correct answer: D

Tailoring matters; one heavy template for every situation usually creates waste.

Question 3 - Hard

An output from Chapter 1 exists, but no role uses it in decisions. What should the BA do?

  1. A. Review its purpose, user, detail level, and link to backlog or decisions
  2. B. Increase the page count so the artifact looks complete
  3. C. Delete the artifact without discussion
  4. D. Pass responsibility to the PM because the BA already produced the document
Show answer

Correct answer: A

A BA artifact has value only when it supports decisions, reduces risk, or guides delivery.

Question 4 - Hard

A team wants to skip Chapter 1 analysis to deliver faster. Which argument is most persuasive?

  1. A. Right-sized analysis reduces late change and does not have to create heavy process
  2. B. All PMI processes must always be fully documented
  3. C. BA must create many documents to prove work was done
  4. D. Without analysis, the project is guaranteed to fail
Show answer

Correct answer: A

The best message balances discipline and speed: enough analysis for risk, not unnecessary ceremony.

Question 5 - Hard

During Chapter 1 review, the BA finds a recorded decision with no assumptions. What is the main risk?

  1. A. The team will not know when the decision should be revisited
  2. B. Stakeholders will always agree with the decision
  3. C. QA never needs assumptions
  4. D. The roadmap will automatically become more accurate
Show answer

Correct answer: A

Assumptions are the conditions that make a decision valid; if they change, the decision may need review.

Question 6 - Hardest

A senior BA is coaching a junior BA on Introduction. Which exercise is hardest and most realistic?

  1. A. Ask the junior BA to memorize every technique name
  2. B. Ask the junior BA to create a very long template

  4. D. Give a vague case and ask for the decision question, technique choice, and trade-off explanation
Show answer

Correct answer: D

BA maturity appears in reasoning, technique selection, and trade-off explanation under ambiguity.

Question 7 - Hardest

If a stakeholder challenges a Chapter 1 analysis result, how should the BA preserve objectivity?

  1. A. Ask for evidence, adjust assumptions, update the decision log, and confirm impact
  2. B. Hide the objection to protect the schedule
  3. C. Accept every objection to avoid conflict
  4. D. Ignore the challenge if the stakeholder is not the sponsor
Show answer

Correct answer: A

A challenge is information. The BA should turn it into evidence, an assumption, or a transparent decision.

Question 8 - Hardest

What characteristic should a good performance measure for Chapter 1 have?

  1. A. It is easy to report but unrelated to outcomes
  2. B. It counts only how many meetings the BA attends
  3. C. It counts only how many pages were written
  4. D. It connects to clarity, rework, cycle time, defects, adoption, or business value

Correct answer: D

Useful measures reflect the outcome of BA work, not only activity volume.

Question 9 - Hardest

When delivery speed conflicts with analysis quality in Chapter 1, what is the best balance?

  2. B. Remove all analysis to protect the sprint
  3. C. Delegate all product decisions to the engineering lead
  4. D. Add a gate approval for every ticket

Correct answer: A

Risk-based analysis goes deeper where uncertainty is dangerous and stays lighter where the work is stable.

Question 10 - Hardest

A solution option in Chapter 1 is preferred by the sponsor but weakly supported by data. What should the BA recommend?

  1. A. Document evidence, assumptions, risk, and propose a small experiment before committing at scale

The BA does not oppose stakeholders; the BA makes evidence and learning options visible.
