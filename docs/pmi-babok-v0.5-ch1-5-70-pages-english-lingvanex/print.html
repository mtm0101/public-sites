Chapter 1: Introduction (Giới thiệu)

Chapter 1 positions business analysis as a discipline that helps an organization understand needs, connect those needs to product information, and evaluate whether a delivered solution creates business value.

1.1 Overview and Purpose of This Guide (Tổng quan và mục đích của hướng dẫn này)

What it is

Establishes a shared frame for understanding and applying business analysis across portfolios, programs, projects, and product work. In practical BA work, this section turns vague observations into structured information that another person can review, test, or use in a product decision.

Why it matters

It reduces the risk that different teams use different meanings for needs, requirements, solutions, and business value. When it is performed well, the team reduces misunderstanding, avoids late rework, and increases the chance that the solution is connected to business value rather than only backlog completion.

When to use it

When an organization is starting to standardize ba capability, launching a new initiative, or aligning several functions around a common way of working. It becomes especially important when assumptions are unvalidated, when multiple stakeholder groups see the situation differently, or when the next decision will consume meaningful resources.

How to apply it

Start with the decision question: what does the team need to know in order to move forward safely? Then identify sources, choose an appropriate technique, document the result at the right level of detail, and confirm the result with people who have authority or direct experience.

  4. Confirm and adjust. Send the result back to the right stakeholders; separate confirmed facts, assumptions, and unresolved decisions.
  5. Connect to delivery. Convert the result into a backlog item, acceptance criteria, roadmap item, risk, action, or metric so it is not lost.

Practical example

In a healthcare software project, a sponsor says that provider search must improve. Product management may think about ranking, engineering may think about Azure Search, and QA may think about test data. A BA uses the purpose of the guide to separate three layers: the business need is helping users find the right provider; product information includes search criteria, data, and constraints; the solution is the set of product changes the team eventually chooses.

Common mistakes

  • reading the guide as a checklist of documents instead of a decision-support framework
  • treating the BA as only a note taker or requirement writer
  • failing to connect BA work to measurable business value

Application checklist

  • Is the problem or opportunity clearly described?
  • Do stakeholders use the same meaning for key terms?
  • Does the BA output support an investment, prioritization, or design decision?
  • Is there a way to evaluate value after release?

In hybrid or predictive delivery, the same information may need a more formal form for approval, audit, or handoff.
