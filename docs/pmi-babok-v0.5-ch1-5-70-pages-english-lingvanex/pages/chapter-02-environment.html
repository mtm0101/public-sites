Chapter 2: The Environment in Which Business Analysis Is Conducted (Môi trường tiến hành phân tích kinh doanh)

Chapter 2 explains why organizational context, process assets, governance, culture, and delivery systems directly influence how a BA elicits, analyzes, communicates, and maintains product information.

Body content is English-only. Titles and terminology remain bilingual.

2.1 Overview (Tổng quan)

What it is

Treats the environment as a set of constraints and opportunities that shape how ba work can be performed. In practical BA work, this section turns vague observations into structured information that another person can review, test, or use in a product decision.

Why it matters

The same technique may work in one organization and fail in another if decision rights, culture, regulation, or operating model differ. When it is performed well, the team reduces misunderstanding, avoids late rework, and increases the chance that the solution is connected to business value rather than only backlog completion.

When to use it

During initiation, when a project changes delivery model, or when barriers appear to come from the organization rather than from the requirements themselves. It becomes especially important when assumptions are unvalidated, when multiple stakeholder groups see the situation differently, or when the next decision will consume meaningful resources.

How to apply it

Start with the decision question: what does the team need to know in order to move forward safely? Then identify sources, choose an appropriate technique, document the result at the right level of detail, and confirm the result with people who have authority or direct experience.

Step-by-step application guide

  1. Frame the question. Write one sentence describing the decision to be supported; do not begin with the name of a preferred solution.
  2. Gather evidence. Combine documents, data, interviews, observations, or workshops to avoid relying on a single opinion.
  3. Structure the information. Use a table, model, story map, RACI, capability view, or decision log to expose relationships and dependencies.
  4. Confirm and adjust. Send the result back to the right stakeholders; separate confirmed facts, assumptions, and unresolved decisions.
  5. Connect to delivery. Convert the result into a backlog item, acceptance criteria, roadmap item, risk, action, or metric so it is not lost.

Practical example

A payment project integrated with Stripe cannot be analyzed like a simple landing page. Security policy, refund operations, transaction logging, sandbox testing, and customer support responsibility all influence the BA plan.

Common mistakes

  • analyzing requirements without understanding real operations
  • treating governance as a late administrative step
  • not identifying who has the real authority to decide

Application checklist

  • Which external factor may change scope?
  • Which team owns the data or core process?
  • Are any internal policies mandatory?
  • What is the organization’s tolerance for risk?

BA lens in Agile or hybrid work

In Agile delivery, this content does not have to become a long document. It can appear in a story map, refinement note, prototype annotation, decision record, or acceptance criteria. In hybrid or predictive delivery, the same information may need a more formal form for approval, audit, or handoff. The quality criterion is not document size; it is whether the information is reliable, current, and useful for the next decision.

Key takeaway: Overview should produce evidence that helps the team decide, prioritize, design, test, or transition. If the artifact is not used, reduce its weight or change how it is presented.

Practical example

In an organization with a strong PMO, the BA may need a clear business case and traceability before a gate approval. In a fast-moving Agile product team, a story map and prototype may produce faster feedback. The content of BA work is similar, but the collaboration form must differ.

Common mistakes

  • using the same communication style for every stakeholder
  • not identifying the true approver
  • allowing product, development, QA, and operations information to become disconnected

Application checklist

  • Who decides scope, budget, and release?
  • What artifacts does the current life cycle require?
  • Are handoff criteria between groups explicit?
  • How should the BA work with PM, PO, QA, UX, and engineering?
