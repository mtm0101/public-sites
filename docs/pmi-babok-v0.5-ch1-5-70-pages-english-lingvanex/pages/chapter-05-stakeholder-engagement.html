5.1 Identify Stakeholders (Xác định các bên liên quan)

What it is

Finds the individuals, groups, and organizations that influence or are influenced by ba outcomes. In practical BA work, this section turns vague observations into structured information that another person can review, test, or use in a product decision.

Why it matters

Missing a key stakeholder often causes incomplete requirements, late changes, or resistance during adoption. When it is performed well, the team reduces misunderstanding, avoids late rework, and increases the chance that the solution is connected to business value rather than only backlog completion.

When to use it

At the start of an initiative, when scope changes, or when an affected user group appears underrepresented. It becomes especially important when assumptions are unvalidated, when multiple stakeholder groups see the situation differently, or when the next decision will consume meaningful resources.

How to apply it

Start with the decision question: what does the team need to know in order to move forward safely? Then identify sources, choose an appropriate technique, document the result at the right level of detail, and confirm the result with people who have authority or direct experience.

Step-by-step application guide

  1. Frame the question. Write one sentence describing the decision to be supported; do not begin with the name of a preferred solution.
  2. Gather evidence. Combine documents, data, interviews, observations, or workshops to avoid relying on a single opinion.
  3. Structure the information. Use a table, model, story map, RACI, capability view, or decision log to expose relationships and dependencies.
  4. Confirm and adjust. Send the result back to the right stakeholders; separate confirmed facts, assumptions, and unresolved decisions.
  5. Connect to delivery. Convert the result into a backlog item, acceptance criteria, roadmap item, risk, action, or metric so it is not lost.

Practical example

For a messaging module, stakeholders are not only message senders. They include recipients, group owners, support, compliance, QA, mobile developers, backend developers, and notification owners. Each group has different risks and questions.

Common mistakes

  • listing only meeting attendees
  • forgetting indirect stakeholders such as support or operations
  • not updating the register when scope changes

Application checklist

  • Are low-power but affected stakeholders included?
  • Are data, system, and process owners identified?
  • Are approvers, operators, and post-release support groups represented?
  • Does the register record role and relevance?

BA lens in Agile or hybrid work

In Agile delivery, this content does not have to become a long document. It can appear in a story map, refinement note, prototype annotation, decision record, or acceptance criteria. In hybrid or predictive delivery, the same information may need a more formal form for approval, audit, or handoff. The quality criterion is not document size; it is whether the information is reliable, current, and useful for the next decision.

Key takeaway: Identify Stakeholders should produce evidence that helps the team decide, prioritize, design, test, or transition. If the artifact is not used, reduce its weight or change how it is presented.

Question 1 - Hard

A Chapter 5 initiative has stakeholders using different meanings for the same product goal. What should the BA do first to reduce rework risk?

  1. A. Write all requirements immediately so engineering can begin
  2. B. Wait for QA to detect gaps after development is complete
  3. C. Create a shared view, confirm assumptions, and connect the output to the next decision
  4. D. Ask only the sponsor because the sponsor has the highest authority
Show answer

Correct answer: C

The strongest action creates clarity and validates assumptions before they become scope.

  4. D. Artifacts are selected according to risk and life cycle

Tailoring matters; one heavy template for every situation usually creates waste.
