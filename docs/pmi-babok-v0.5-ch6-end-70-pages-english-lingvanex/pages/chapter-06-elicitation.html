Chapter 6: Elicitation (Sự khơi gợi)

Chapter 6 explains how a business analyst plans, prepares, conducts, and confirms elicitation so that stakeholder knowledge becomes usable product information rather than unverified conversation notes.

Body content is English only. Titles and terminology remain bilingual.

6.1 Determine Elicitation Approach (Xác định phương pháp thu thập thông tin)

What it is

Section 6-1 defines how information will be obtained, from whom, in what sequence, and with what level of formality before detailed elicitation begins. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

A deliberate approach prevents the ba from overusing interviews, missing silent stakeholder groups, or collecting information that cannot be validated later. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it before discovery workshops, interviews, surveys, observations, prototype reviews, or any situation where stakeholder knowledge must be converted into product decisions. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 6-1 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Name the decision that this section must support before choosing an artifact.
  2. Gather evidence from product information, stakeholder knowledge, models, or performance data.
  3. Separate facts, assumptions, open questions, and decisions so reviewers can challenge the right layer.
  4. Create a lightweight artifact that is strong enough to support delivery, testing, traceability, or release.
  5. Review the result with the correct owner and record how the team will use it next.

Practical software example

For a provider-search product, the BA may combine stakeholder interviews with observation of support tickets, prototype walkthroughs with clinicians, and a survey for beta users. The approach explains why each technique is selected, what information it should produce, and how the result will be confirmed.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In an adaptive delivery environment, treat this section as a repeated learning loop rather than a single documentation milestone. The BA should keep the artifact small enough to update, yet rigorous enough that important trade-offs remain visible. For section 6-1, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Starting with a technique before defining the decision that information must support
  • Inviting only convenient stakeholders and overlooking operations, compliance, support, or edge-case users
  • Treating the elicitation plan as fixed even when early sessions reveal a different source of truth

Application checklist

  • Can each elicitation session be tied to a specific decision or information gap?
  • Have high-influence and high-impact stakeholders both been considered?
  • Is there a plan to confirm results after collection?
  • Does the chosen technique match the kind of information needed: behavior, rule, decision, data, pain point, or preference?

Cross-functional collaboration notes

Product owners use the result of section 6-1 to make priority and scope decisions. Developers use it to understand behavior, data, and constraints. QA uses it to design tests and identify acceptance evidence. Operations, support, compliance, or customer success use it to prepare for real-world use. The BA should make these handoffs explicit rather than assuming that one artifact works equally well for every audience.

Key takeaway: Determine Elicitation Approach (Xác định phương pháp thu thập thông tin) matters when it creates evidence that changes a decision, reduces product risk, clarifies stakeholder intent, or proves that a solution is ready to move forward.

6.2 Prepare for Elicitation (Chuẩn bị cho việc thu thập thông tin)

What it is

Section 6-2 turns the elicitation approach into concrete materials, questions, scenarios, agendas, logistics, and working context. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Well-prepared elicitation shortens meetings, improves stakeholder focus, reduces ambiguity, and prevents the ba from using valuable stakeholder time to search for basic background information. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it before any planned elicitation activity where participants need shared context or where the ba needs structured evidence. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 6-2 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Start with the risk of being wrong: identify what would break if the team misunderstood this topic.
  2. Select the technique that best reveals the needed evidence rather than the technique that is most familiar.
  3. Use examples and counterexamples to test whether stakeholders share the same interpretation.
  4. Update requirements, models, criteria, trace links, or decisions so the insight becomes usable.
  5. Revisit the artifact when new information changes the original assumption.

Practical software example

Before a workshop on subscription management, the BA prepares current workflow screenshots, known defect patterns, payment states, draft scenarios, open assumptions, and a decision list. This lets stakeholders discuss behavior and trade-offs instead of spending the session reconstructing context.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a hybrid program, this section often connects informal discovery with formal governance. The BA may use a lightweight working artifact during refinement and later summarize key decisions for approval, audit, or transition planning. For section 6-2, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Preparing a long agenda that does not state the decision to be made
  • Failing to review existing requirements, analytics, defects, or prior lessons before asking stakeholders repetitive questions
  • Using one set of questions for every stakeholder group even when each group owns different knowledge

Application checklist

  • Is the objective visible at the top of the agenda or interview guide?
  • Are open assumptions separated from known facts?
  • Have participants received the background they need before the session?
  • Is there a capture method for decisions, unresolved questions, and follow-up owners?

Product owners use the result of section 6-2 to make priority and scope decisions. Developers use it to understand behavior, data, and constraints. 6.3 Conduct Elicitation (Tiến hành thu thập thông tin)

What it is

Section 6-3 collects stakeholder knowledge, behavioral evidence, business rules, constraints, data needs, and user expectations through the selected techniques. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

The product team cannot analyze or validate what it has not first discovered; elicitation creates the raw material for requirements, models, risks, and acceptance criteria. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it when the ba needs to understand actual work, decision logic, user goals, exceptions, operational constraints, or stakeholder priorities. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 6-3 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Define the audience: sponsor, product owner, developer, tester, operations, compliance, or end user.
  2. Choose the minimum detail that lets that audience make a safe and informed decision.
  3. Show trade-offs explicitly so the team does not treat analysis as a yes/no formality.
  4. Make dependencies visible early instead of discovering them during testing or release.
  5. Convert unresolved issues into follow-up actions with owners and dates.

Practical software example

When investigating why users abandon onboarding, the BA may observe real sessions, ask users to think aloud on a prototype, interview support staff, and review analytics. Each technique reveals a different kind of evidence: behavior, perception, root cause, or scale.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a regulated or high-risk product, this section deserves stronger evidence and clearer ownership. The BA should document the source of information, decision criteria, and trace links because the cost of misunderstanding is higher. For section 6-3, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Treating the loudest voice in a workshop as the final truth
  • Capturing statements without context, source, confidence, or follow-up status
  • Mixing solution ideas, business needs, and design constraints in one undifferentiated notes document

Application checklist

  • Were facts, assumptions, opinions, and decisions tagged separately?
  • Did the BA probe exceptions rather than only the happy path?
  • Were stakeholders asked for examples instead of abstract preferences only?
  • Are unconfirmed results clearly marked as unconfirmed until reviewed?

Cross-functional collaboration notes

Product owners use the result of section 6-3 to make priority and scope decisions. Developers use it to understand behavior, data, and constraints. QA uses it to design tests and identify acceptance evidence. Operations, support, compliance, or customer success use it to prepare for real-world use. 6.4 Confirm Elicitation Results (Xác nhận kết quả thu thập thông tin)

What it is

Section 6-4 checks whether the captured elicitation results accurately represent stakeholder intent before they are analyzed, modeled, or converted into requirements. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Confirmation reduces the risk of building requirements from misunderstood notes, translated terminology, incomplete examples, or assumptions that stakeholders never accepted. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it after interviews, workshops, observation sessions, prototype reviews, surveys, or any activity where the ba captured information for future use. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 6-4 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Identify which product outcome this work protects: value, adoption, usability, compliance, operability, or quality.
  2. Connect the activity to concrete product information such as requirements, acceptance criteria, models, risks, or KPIs.
  3. Use review sessions to uncover disagreement, not merely to collect approval.
  4. Document rationale in a decision log so future teams can understand why an option was chosen.
  5. Keep the artifact living; retire it when it no longer supports a decision.

Practical software example

After a care-coordination workshop, the BA sends a concise confirmation package: decisions made, unresolved questions, workflow assumptions, data elements, and example scenarios. Stakeholders can correct terminology, add missing exceptions, or confirm that the summary is ready for analysis.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a fast-moving product team, the risk is not lack of activity; it is unstructured activity. The BA should convert conversations into reusable product information without slowing the team with unnecessary ceremony. For section 6-4, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Assuming silence means agreement
  • Sending a transcript instead of a structured summary that stakeholders can review efficiently
  • Failing to record who confirmed which information and which items remain open

Application checklist

  • Is the confirmation format short enough for stakeholders to review?
  • Are disputed points separated from accepted points?
  • Does each confirmed item have a source or responsible stakeholder?
  • Are corrections fed back into the product information repository?

Cross-functional collaboration notes

Product owners use the result of section 6-4 to make priority and scope decisions. Developers use it to understand behavior, data, and constraints. QA uses it to design tests and identify acceptance evidence. Operations, support, compliance, or customer success use it to prepare for real-world use. The BA should make these handoffs explicit rather than assuming that one artifact works equally well for every audience.
