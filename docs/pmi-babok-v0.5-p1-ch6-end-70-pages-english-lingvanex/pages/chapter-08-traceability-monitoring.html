Chapter 8: Traceability and Monitoring (Truy xuất nguồn gốc và giám sát)

Chapter 8 focuses on maintaining the relationships among needs, goals, requirements, designs, tests, changes, and outcomes so that the product team can understand impact and preserve value as information evolves.

8.1 Determine Traceability and Monitoring Approach (Xác định phương pháp truy xuất nguồn gốc và giám sát)

What it is

Section 8-1 defines how relationships, changes, approvals, versions, and monitoring signals will be captured and maintained. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Without a traceability approach, a change to one requirement can silently break business goals, test coverage, design decisions, release plans, or compliance evidence. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it before requirements are baselined, when products have many dependencies, or when regulatory, configuration, or change-control expectations are high. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 8-1 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Start with the risk of being wrong: identify what would break if the team misunderstood this topic.
  2. Select the technique that best reveals the needed evidence rather than the technique that is most familiar.
  3. Use examples and counterexamples to test whether stakeholders share the same interpretation.
  4. Update requirements, models, criteria, trace links, or decisions so the insight becomes usable.
  5. Revisit the artifact when new information changes the original assumption.

Practical software example

A healthcare product may trace business goals to requirements, requirements to acceptance criteria, acceptance criteria to test cases, and release changes to risk decisions. The approach should also define what is too small to trace.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a hybrid program, this section often connects informal discovery with formal governance. The BA may use a lightweight working artifact during refinement and later summarize key decisions for approval, audit, or transition planning. For section 8-1, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Tracing everything with the same intensity and making the repository unusable
  • Tracing only after development, when impact analysis is already too late
  • Using a tool without defining relationship types and update responsibilities

Application checklist

  • Which relationships must be visible for decision making?
  • Who updates trace links when requirements change?
  • How will the team know whether traceability is stale?
  • What level of trace detail is justified by risk and governance needs?

Cross-functional collaboration notes

Product owners use the result of section 8-1 to make priority and scope decisions. Developers use it to understand behavior, data, and constraints. QA uses it to design tests and identify acceptance evidence. Operations, support, compliance, or customer success use it to prepare for real-world use. The BA should make these handoffs explicit rather than assuming that one artifact works equally well for every audience.

Key takeaway: Determine Traceability and Monitoring Approach (Xác định phương pháp truy xuất nguồn gốc và giám sát) matters when it creates evidence that changes a decision, reduces product risk, clarifies stakeholder intent, or proves that a solution is ready to move forward.

8.2 Establish Relationships and Dependencies (Thiết lập các mối quan hệ và sự phụ thuộc)

What it is

Section 8-2 links product information so the team can see how needs, requirements, models, risks, tests, stakeholders, and release items depend on one another. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Relationship visibility enables impact analysis, conflict detection, dependency planning, and responsible change decisions. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it when requirements are being elaborated, prioritized, approved, changed, or connected to design and testing artifacts. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 8-2 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Define the audience: sponsor, product owner, developer, tester, operations, compliance, or end user.
  2. Choose the minimum detail that lets that audience make a safe and informed decision.
  3. Show trade-offs explicitly so the team does not treat analysis as a yes/no formality.
  4. Make dependencies visible early instead of discovering them during testing or release.
  5. Convert unresolved issues into follow-up actions with owners and dates.

Practical software example

A requirement for filtering by insurance may depend on provider data, payer data, UI design, search indexing, data refresh frequency, and acceptance criteria for no-match behavior. Linking these elements prevents a data change from appearing isolated when it affects the user experience.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a regulated or high-risk product, this section deserves stronger evidence and clearer ownership. The BA should document the source of information, decision criteria, and trace links because the cost of misunderstanding is higher. For section 8-2, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Creating links that are too vague to support impact analysis
  • Failing to include non-functional dependencies such as security, performance, or data retention
  • Updating the requirement text but not the linked tests or models

Application checklist

  • Can the team trace a requirement back to a need or goal?
  • Can the team trace a change forward to design, test, risk, and release impact?
  • Are dependency owners known?
  • Are external systems and data sources included where they matter?

Cross-functional collaboration notes

8.3 Select and Approve Requirements (Lựa chọn và phê duyệt yêu cầu)

What it is

Section 8-3 establishes which requirements and product information are accepted for delivery, baseline, release, or further analysis. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Approval creates an explicit decision point so stakeholders know what is in scope, what is deferred, and what criteria governed the decision. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it before sprint commitment, release scope confirmation, formal baseline, change-control review, or vendor handoff. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 8-3 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Identify which product outcome this work protects: value, adoption, usability, compliance, operability, or quality.
  2. Connect the activity to concrete product information such as requirements, acceptance criteria, models, risks, or KPIs.
  3. Use review sessions to uncover disagreement, not merely to collect approval.
  4. Document rationale in a decision log so future teams can understand why an option was chosen.
  5. Keep the artifact living; retire it when it no longer supports a decision.

Practical software example

Before a release, the team confirms that the selected requirements have valid acceptance criteria, manageable dependencies, aligned stakeholders, and understood product risks. Items that fail the readiness standard are refined or deferred.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a fast-moving product team, the risk is not lack of activity; it is unstructured activity. The BA should convert conversations into reusable product information without slowing the team with unnecessary ceremony. For section 8-3, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Treating approval as a signature rather than a decision based on evidence
  • Approving requirements that are verified but not validated against business goals
  • Allowing unclear ownership to block approval or create informal scope changes

Application checklist

  • Are selected requirements both verified and validated?
  • Have dependencies and risks been reviewed before approval?
  • Is the approval authority clear?
  • Are deferred items and decision reasons visible?

8.4 Manage Changes to Requirements and Other Product Information (Quản lý thay đổi yêu cầu và thông tin sản phẩm khác)

What it is

Section 8-4 controls how requirements and related product information change after they have been selected, approved, baselined, or used for delivery. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Change is normal, but unmanaged change breaks alignment among goals, requirements, design, tests, risks, and stakeholder expectations. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it when new information, defects, stakeholder feedback, compliance changes, or design discoveries affect existing product information. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 8-4 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Name the decision that this section must support before choosing an artifact.
  2. Gather evidence from product information, stakeholder knowledge, models, or performance data.
  3. Separate facts, assumptions, open questions, and decisions so reviewers can challenge the right layer.
  4. Create a lightweight artifact that is strong enough to support delivery, testing, traceability, or release.
  5. Review the result with the correct owner and record how the team will use it next.

Practical software example

If beta users ask for a different search result layout, the BA checks affected requirements, usability assumptions, analytics events, mobile layout constraints, test cases, training material, and release notes before recommending whether the change should be accepted now or scheduled later.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In an adaptive delivery environment, treat this section as a repeated learning loop rather than a single documentation milestone. The BA should keep the artifact small enough to update, yet rigorous enough that important trade-offs remain visible. For section 8-4, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Treating all change as scope creep and losing learning opportunities
  • Accepting change without impact analysis because the request appears small
  • Updating the backlog but not models, acceptance criteria, trace links, or stakeholder communication

Application checklist

  • What goal or problem does the change support?
  • Which approved items, tests, data, and release plans are affected?
  • Who has authority to accept or reject the change?
  • Has the decision been recorded with rationale and follow-up actions?

Cross-functional collaboration notes
