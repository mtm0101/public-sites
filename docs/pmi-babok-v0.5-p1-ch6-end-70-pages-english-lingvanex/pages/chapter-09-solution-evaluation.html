Chapter 9: Solution Evaluation (Đánh giá giải pháp)

Chapter 9 explains how the business analyst helps evaluate whether a solution performs, whether acceptance results support release decisions, and whether remaining defects or risks require action.

Body content is English only. Titles and terminology remain bilingual.

9.1 Evaluate Solution Performance (Đánh giá hiệu suất giải pháp)

What it is

Section 9-1 assesses whether an implemented or partially implemented solution is delivering the expected business value and operational results. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

A solution can pass requirements and still fail to create value; performance evaluation closes the loop between investment intent and real outcomes. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it after pilot, release, beta, production launch, or any point where usage, operational, financial, or stakeholder feedback is available. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 9-1 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Start with the risk of being wrong: identify what would break if the team misunderstood this topic.
  2. Select the technique that best reveals the needed evidence rather than the technique that is most familiar.
  3. Use examples and counterexamples to test whether stakeholders share the same interpretation.
  4. Update requirements, models, criteria, trace links, or decisions so the insight becomes usable.
  5. Revisit the artifact when new information changes the original assumption.

Practical software example

After launching improved provider search, the BA reviews usage, result-click rate, no-result frequency, support tickets, stakeholder interviews, and downstream appointment behavior. The evaluation may show that ranking improved but users still need better explanations for why a provider matched.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a hybrid program, this section often connects informal discovery with formal governance. The BA may use a lightweight working artifact during refinement and later summarize key decisions for approval, audit, or transition planning. For section 9-1, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Measuring only delivery completion instead of business value
  • Using metrics that are available rather than metrics that matter
  • Failing to separate adoption problems from solution capability problems

Application checklist

  • Which benefit from the business case is being measured?
  • Do performance data and stakeholder feedback tell the same story?
  • Are underperforming areas tied to root causes?
  • Does the assessment recommend action, further measurement, or closure?

Cross-functional collaboration notes

Product owners use the result of section 9-1 to make priority and scope decisions. Developers use it to understand behavior, data, and constraints. QA uses it to design tests and identify acceptance evidence. Operations, support, compliance, or customer success use it to prepare for real-world use. The BA should make these handoffs explicit rather than assuming that one artifact works equally well for every audience.

Key takeaway: Evaluate Solution Performance (Đánh giá hiệu suất giải pháp) matters when it creates evidence that changes a decision, reduces product risk, clarifies stakeholder intent, or proves that a solution is ready to move forward.

9.2 Determine Solution Evaluation Approach (Xác định phương pháp đánh giá giải pháp)

What it is

Section 9-2 defines what will be measured, when, by whom, using what data sources, and how the result will be interpreted. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Evaluation must be planned before release; otherwise the team may discover too late that the necessary data or baseline was never captured. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it during planning, analysis, acceptance preparation, pilot design, or before instrumenting analytics and operational reporting. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 9-2 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Define the audience: sponsor, product owner, developer, tester, operations, compliance, or end user.
  2. Choose the minimum detail that lets that audience make a safe and informed decision.
  3. Show trade-offs explicitly so the team does not treat analysis as a yes/no formality.
  4. Make dependencies visible early instead of discovering them during testing or release.
  5. Convert unresolved issues into follow-up actions with owners and dates.

Practical software example

For an onboarding redesign, the approach may define conversion rate, time to complete, support contacts, error rate, user satisfaction, and drop-off points. It also states where data comes from and how long the team will wait before judging performance.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a regulated or high-risk product, this section deserves stronger evidence and clearer ownership. The BA should document the source of information, decision criteria, and trace links because the cost of misunderstanding is higher. For section 9-2, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Defining KPIs after launch when baseline data is missing
  • Choosing too many metrics and creating noise rather than insight
  • Ignoring stakeholders who own the systems that collect performance data

Application checklist

  • Are measures connected to business goals?
  • Is a baseline available or planned?
  • Who owns each metric and data source?
  • What threshold will trigger action or investigation?

9.3 Evaluate Acceptance Results and Address Defects (Đánh giá kết quả chấp nhận và khắc phục lỗi)

What it is

Section 9-3 interprets actual acceptance results against acceptance criteria and helps decide what to do with defects, gaps, or variances. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

The ba supports informed release decisions by analyzing severity, root cause, related risks, and product impact instead of simply counting failed tests. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it during uat, beta, acceptance testing, pilot review, or any period where evidence shows the solution may not satisfy agreed criteria. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 9-3 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Identify which product outcome this work protects: value, adoption, usability, compliance, operability, or quality.
  2. Connect the activity to concrete product information such as requirements, acceptance criteria, models, risks, or KPIs.
  3. Use review sessions to uncover disagreement, not merely to collect approval.
  4. Document rationale in a decision log so future teams can understand why an option was chosen.
  5. Keep the artifact living; retire it when it no longer supports a decision.

Practical software example

If a provider card passes visual tests but fails accessibility checks and displays stale availability data, the BA helps distinguish UI defect, data quality problem, acceptance criteria gap, and release-blocking risk.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a fast-moving product team, the risk is not lack of activity; it is unstructured activity. The BA should convert conversations into reusable product information without slowing the team with unnecessary ceremony. For section 9-3, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Treating every failed acceptance result as the same priority
  • Fixing symptoms without identifying whether the requirement, design, data, or implementation caused the variance
  • Letting defect triage occur without business context

Application checklist

  • Which acceptance criterion did the result fail?
  • Is the issue a defect, missed requirement, changed expectation, or test-data problem?
  • What user or business impact does it create?
  • Does the defect block release, require workaround, or become future backlog work?

9.4 Obtain Solution Acceptance for Release (Nhận được sự chấp thuận giải pháp để phát hành)

What it is

Section 9-4 supports the go/no-go decision by confirming that the solution, acceptance evidence, risks, readiness, and transition needs are understood. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Release acceptance is a business decision, not only a technical deployment decision; stakeholders need to understand remaining trade-offs. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it before production launch, pilot expansion, major release, or any deployment that affects users, operations, compliance, or customer commitments. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 9-4 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Name the decision that this section must support before choosing an artifact.
  2. Gather evidence from product information, stakeholder knowledge, models, or performance data.
  3. Separate facts, assumptions, open questions, and decisions so reviewers can challenge the right layer.
  4. Create a lightweight artifact that is strong enough to support delivery, testing, traceability, or release.
  5. Review the result with the correct owner and record how the team will use it next.

Practical software example

Before releasing a new payment-management flow, stakeholders review accepted requirements, defect status, rollback options, app-store constraints, support readiness, analytics, customer communication, and unresolved product risks. The BA helps make the decision explicit and traceable.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In an adaptive delivery environment, treat this section as a repeated learning loop rather than a single documentation milestone. The BA should keep the artifact small enough to update, yet rigorous enough that important trade-offs remain visible. For section 9-4, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Reducing release approval to “testing passed” while ignoring readiness and residual risk
  • Failing to document conditions attached to a conditional release
  • Not communicating release decision rationale to affected stakeholder groups

Application checklist

  • Do decision makers understand accepted scope and known gaps?
  • Are transition and support plans ready?
  • Have product risks and mitigations been reviewed?
  • Is the release decision recorded with rationale, owners, and follow-up actions?

Cross-functional collaboration notes

Product owners use the result of section 9-4 to make priority and scope decisions. Developers use it to understand behavior, data, and constraints. QA uses it to design tests and identify acceptance evidence. Operations, support, compliance, or customer success use it to prepare for real-world use. The BA should make these handoffs explicit rather than assuming that one artifact works equally well for every audience.
