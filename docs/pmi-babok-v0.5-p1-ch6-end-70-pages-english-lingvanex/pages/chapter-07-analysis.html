7.1 Determine Analysis Approach (Xác định phương pháp phân tích)

What it is

Section 7-1 sets the method for analyzing elicitation results and deciding which models, artifacts, reviews, and quality checks will be used. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Analysis without an approach can become inconsistent, overly document-heavy, or too informal to support traceability and decision making. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it when the ba is moving from discovery into structured product information, especially before detailed modeling or story elaboration. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 7-1 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Name the decision that this section must support before choosing an artifact.
  2. Gather evidence from product information, stakeholder knowledge, models, or performance data.
  3. Separate facts, assumptions, open questions, and decisions so reviewers can challenge the right layer.
  4. Create a lightweight artifact that is strong enough to support delivery, testing, traceability, or release.
  5. Review the result with the correct owner and record how the team will use it next.

Practical software example

For a mobile subscription workflow, the BA may decide that state modeling, user stories, acceptance criteria, and a decision log are enough for Agile delivery, while the payment integration also needs data tables and compliance traceability.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In an adaptive delivery environment, treat this section as a repeated learning loop rather than a single documentation milestone. The BA should keep the artifact small enough to update, yet rigorous enough that important trade-offs remain visible. For section 7-1, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Selecting models because they are familiar rather than because they answer the current analysis question
  • Allowing each feature team to define requirement quality differently
  • Skipping analysis planning because the project is Agile

Application checklist

  • Does the approach explain which models will be used and why?
  • Does it define when requirements are ready for elaboration or review?
  • Does it connect analysis work to traceability and acceptance?
  • Is the level of formality proportional to risk and complexity?

Cross-functional collaboration notes

Product owners use the result of section 7-1 to make priority and scope decisions. Developers use it to understand behavior, data, and constraints. QA uses it to design tests and identify acceptance evidence. Operations, support, compliance, or customer success use it to prepare for real-world use. The BA should make these handoffs explicit rather than assuming that one artifact works equally well for every audience.

Key takeaway: Determine Analysis Approach (Xác định phương pháp phân tích) matters when it creates evidence that changes a decision, reduces product risk, clarifies stakeholder intent, or proves that a solution is ready to move forward.

7.2 Create and Analyze Models (Tạo và phân tích mô hình)

What it is

Section 7-2 uses visual or structured representations to reveal relationships, gaps, dependencies, decisions, data, states, workflows, and interface behavior. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Models make invisible complexity visible; they help stakeholders catch misunderstandings earlier than long text requirements usually do. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it when a process, data structure, rule set, state transition, ecosystem, goal hierarchy, or user interaction cannot be understood reliably through prose alone. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 7-2 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Start with the risk of being wrong: identify what would break if the team misunderstood this topic.
  2. Select the technique that best reveals the needed evidence rather than the technique that is most familiar.
  3. Use examples and counterexamples to test whether stakeholders share the same interpretation.
  4. Update requirements, models, criteria, trace links, or decisions so the insight becomes usable.
  5. Revisit the artifact when new information changes the original assumption.

Practical software example

A state diagram for subscription status can expose scenarios hidden in text: trial, active, payment failed, grace period, cancelled, refunded, and expired. That model helps Product, Dev, QA, and Support agree on expected behavior before implementation.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

For section 7-2, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Creating beautiful diagrams that no decision maker uses
  • Modeling the happy path while ignoring exceptions, states, and handoffs
  • Failing to reconcile models with each other, such as a process model that contradicts a data flow

Application checklist

  • Which question does each model answer?
  • Can stakeholders review the model without expert translation?
  • Are exceptions, alternate flows, and boundary conditions visible?
  • Has the model been linked to requirements or decisions?

7.3 Define and Elaborate Requirements (Xác định và làm rõ các yêu cầu)

What it is

Section 7-3 turns confirmed information and analysis models into clear, testable, useful requirements and related product information. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Requirements must be understandable enough for design, development, testing, prioritization, and stakeholder validation; vague statements create rework. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it after enough elicitation and analysis have occurred to describe what the product must do, under what conditions, and for what business reason. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

  2. Connect the activity to concrete product information such as requirements, acceptance criteria, models, risks, or KPIs.
  3. Use review sessions to uncover disagreement, not merely to collect approval.
  4. Document rationale in a decision log so future teams can understand why an option was chosen.
  5. Keep the artifact living; retire it when it no longer supports a decision.

Practical software example

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Leaving edge cases to QA after development has already started
  • Writing criteria that cannot be tested or demonstrated

Application checklist

  • Do the criteria describe observable behavior?
  • Are error states, permissions, and empty states included when relevant?
  • Can each criterion be tested independently?
  • Do the criteria support the intended solution evaluation approach?

7.8 Identify and Analyze Product Risks (Xác định và phân tích rủi ro sản phẩm)

What it is

Section 7-8 identifies uncertain conditions that could prevent the product from delivering value, being accepted, complying with constraints, or operating successfully. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Product risk analysis shifts attention from schedule alone to the possibility that the solution itself may fail users, data, compliance, operations, or business outcomes. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it when assumptions are high, data is unreliable, integrations are complex, users are changing behavior, or a feature affects regulated or critical workflows. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 7-8 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

Step-by-step application pattern

  1. Identify which product outcome this work protects: value, adoption, usability, compliance, operability, or quality.
  2. Connect the activity to concrete product information such as requirements, acceptance criteria, models, risks, or KPIs.
  3. Use review sessions to uncover disagreement, not merely to collect approval.
  4. Document rationale in a decision log so future teams can understand why an option was chosen.
  5. Keep the artifact living; retire it when it no longer supports a decision.

Practical software example

For an AI-driven search enhancement, product risks may include poor ranking quality, biased results, lack of explainability, missing fallback when data is sparse, and stakeholder distrust if results cannot be justified.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In a fast-moving product team, the risk is not lack of activity; it is unstructured activity. The BA should convert conversations into reusable product information without slowing the team with unnecessary ceremony. For section 7-8, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Recording only project risks such as timeline and staffing while ignoring product adoption or solution-fit risks
  • Treating risks as negative statements without probability, impact, owner, or response
  • Failing to connect high product risk to experiments or acceptance criteria

Application checklist

  • Is the risk about product value, usability, data, compliance, operations, or acceptance?
  • Does each major risk have an owner and response?
  • Are risky assumptions tested early?
  • Do acceptance criteria or metrics reflect the most important risks?

The BA should make these handoffs explicit rather than assuming that one artifact works equally well for every audience.

7.9 Assess Product Design Options (Đánh giá các lựa chọn thiết kế sản phẩm)

What it is

Section 7-9 compares feasible design options before the product team commits to one solution path. It turns business analysis from a vague activity into a controlled way of producing reliable product information.

Why it is used

Different designs create different trade-offs in usability, cost, scalability, implementation risk, operations, and business fit. Used well, it reduces rework and gives stakeholders a clearer basis for decisions.

When to use it

Use it when several solution approaches can satisfy the same requirement or when stakeholders are choosing among build, buy, configure, integrate, or defer options. The section becomes especially important when decisions are expensive to reverse.

How to apply it

Apply section 7-9 by making the decision context explicit, selecting evidence sources, producing a reviewable artifact, and linking the result to delivery, acceptance, traceability, or evaluation.

  4. Create a lightweight artifact that is strong enough to support delivery, testing, traceability, or release.

Practical software example

For provider recommendations, options may include a rule-based ranking model, vector search, hybrid ranking, guided filters, or an AI assistant. The BA helps compare them using business goals, data availability, explainability, effort, and user trust.

Tailoring and lifecycle lens

In an adaptive delivery environment, treat this section as a repeated learning loop rather than a single documentation milestone. The BA should keep the artifact small enough to update, yet rigorous enough that important trade-offs remain visible. For section 7-9, the right level of detail depends on product complexity, stakeholder availability, regulatory expectations, and the cost of downstream change.

Common mistakes / anti-patterns

  • Treating design choice as purely technical when it has adoption and business consequences
  • Ignoring the option to test with a prototype before full investment
  • Failing to document why an option was rejected

  • Are options compared against agreed criteria?
  • Are constraints and assumptions visible?
  • Has the team considered small experiments or staged delivery?
  • Can the recommended design be traced to the requirements and risks it addresses?
